Section 1

What is Toe Be Sure?

A quick introduction to the platform and the problem it solves.

🦶 The Problem

Diabetic Foot Ulcers (DFUs) are open sores or wounds that appear on the feet of people with diabetes. They affect roughly 15% of all diabetic patients and, if left undetected, can lead to serious infections, hospitalisations, and even amputations. Early detection is critical — but many patients don't have immediate access to specialist doctors.

✅ The Solution — Toe Be Sure

Toe Be Sure (TBS) is a free web application that uses Artificial Intelligence (AI) to analyse a photograph of a foot and give an instant, explainable risk assessment for diabetic foot ulcers. Think of it as a "second opinion" tool that can be accessed from any device with a browser.

🏥

Who is it for?

Doctors, nurses, caregivers, and diabetic patients who want a fast initial screening tool.

🌍

Where does it run?

Entirely in your web browser at toe-be-sure.vercel.app. No app to install.

🔒

Is it private?

Images are sent only for analysis and not stored permanently. Login is secured via Google.

⚡

How fast is it?

Results appear within a few seconds of clicking "Analyze Image".

Logging In

TBS requires a Google Sign-In to protect user privacy. Here's how to do it.

1

Open the website

In your browser, go to https://toe-be-sure.vercel.app. You will see a loading spinner for a moment while the session is checked.

2

You're redirected to the Sign-In page

If you are not logged in, the site automatically takes you to a login screen. You will see a "Sign in with Google" button.

3

Click "Sign in with Google"

A Google pop-up will appear. Choose the Google account you want to use, or enter your Gmail address and password.

4

You're in!

After Google confirms your identity, you are returned to the main TBS dashboard. Your name and profile picture from Google will appear in the top-right corner of the navigation bar.

🔑 First-time users: The website may ask for your permission to access basic Google profile information (name, email, and photo). This is safe and is only used to personalise your session — TBS does not share your data with anyone.

Signing Out

Click your profile picture or name in the top-right corner of the navigation bar, then click "Sign out". On mobile, open the hamburger menu (☰) to find the Sign out button.

Section 5

Running Your First Analysis

A complete step-by-step guide from uploading a photo to getting your result.

1

Go to the Analysis Tool

Click "Start Analysis" on the hero section, or click "How It Works" in the navigation bar. The page will smoothly scroll to the two-column analysis area.

2

Upload a Foot Image

You will see a dashed upload box labelled "📷 Foot Image" on the left. You can either:

  • Drag and drop a photo from your computer directly into the box, OR
  • Click the box to open your file browser and select an image

Accepted formats: JPG, PNG, WEBP. The image preview will appear in the box immediately after selection.

3

Remove the image (if needed)

If you uploaded the wrong photo, click the red "✕ Remove image" button that appears below the preview to clear it and start again.

4

Fill in Clinical Parameters (optional, but recommended)

Below the upload box is a second card labelled "🩺 Clinical Parameters". These fields are optional — the AI can run on the image alone — but filling them in makes the SHAP chart (see Section 6) more personalised and informative.

  • Age — patient's age in years (e.g. 62)
  • BMI — Body Mass Index in kg/m² (e.g. 31.2)
  • Diabetes Duration — how many years the patient has had diabetes
  • Fasting Blood Sugar — blood glucose in mg/dL (e.g. 180)
  • Previous Ulcers / Wounds — dropdown: No / Yes – healed / Yes – active
5

Click "🔍 Analyze Image"

The large teal button at the bottom of the left column. It will be greyed out until you upload an image. Once clicked:

  • The button label changes to "Running AI inference…" with a spinning icon
  • On the right side, a loading animation appears
  • The analysis usually takes 2–8 seconds
6

View your results on the right panel

Four result cards will appear on the right side of the screen. See Section 6 for a detailed breakdown of what each card means.

⚠️ If the "Analyze Image" button stays greyed out: Make sure you have successfully uploaded an image. The button only activates when an image is present in the upload box.
💡 No AI backend connected? If the live AI server (Colab backend) is not running, TBS automatically uses a simulation mode to generate a plausible sample result. Simulated results are labelled with "⚡ Simulated" at the bottom of the Risk card. They are for demonstration only and should not be used for medical decisions.

Section 6

Reading Your Results

After the analysis runs, four result cards appear on the right. Here is exactly what each one means.

Card 1: AI Prediction & Risk Level

This is the primary result — a clear verdict with a colour-coded risk badge.

🔴 High Risk
🟡 Medium Risk
🟢 Low Risk
Risk Level AI Label What it means Recommended action
🔴 High DFU Detected The AI detected patterns strongly consistent with an active diabetic foot ulcer. Ulcer probability ≥ 65%. Seek medical attention immediately. Visit a doctor or wound care specialist as soon as possible.
🟡 Medium DFU Risk Detected Early-stage or pre-ulcerative changes were identified. Ulcer probability is between 35%–65%. See a healthcare professional soon. Preventive care and regular monitoring are strongly advised.
🟢 Low No DFU Detected No significant ulcer patterns found. Tissue appears within normal clinical range. Ulcer probability < 35%. Continue routine monitoring. Maintain foot hygiene and schedule regular check-ups.

Model Confidence — the horizontal bar below the badge shows how "sure" the AI is about its prediction, expressed as a percentage (e.g. 87.3%). A higher percentage means the model found clearer evidence for its decision.

Card 2: 🌡️ Grad-CAM Visual Explanation

This card displays your uploaded foot photo with a colour overlay (heatmap) showing exactly which parts of the image the AI focused on to make its decision.

  • Bright red / orange patches — areas of highest concern; the AI sees potential ulcer-related patterns here
  • Yellow / light patches — moderate activation areas
  • Dark / no overlay — areas the AI considered low-risk or ignored
  • Green patches (Low risk result) — minimal activation, looks healthy
Think of it like: a doctor circling the problem areas on an X-ray. The heatmap shows you where the AI looked, not just what it decided. This is called Explainable AI (XAI).

Technical note: When the live AI backend is connected, these heatmaps are generated by the real ResNet18 model using Grad-CAM. When in simulation mode, they are drawn as approximations.

Card 3: 📊 SHAP Feature Importance

This chart answers: "Which patient factors most influenced this risk result?" It uses a technique called SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) — a standard method from data science for explaining AI decisions.

  • Each bar represents a clinical factor (Age, BMI, Diabetes Duration, Blood Sugar, Previous Wounds)
  • Red bars (positive values) — this factor increases the risk prediction
  • Green bars (negative values) — this factor reduces the risk prediction
  • Longer bar = stronger influence on the result
Example: If a patient had diabetes for 20 years and their blood sugar is 300 mg/dL, both bars will be long and red, pushing the risk higher. If they're young and have never had wounds, those bars will be short and green.

Note: If you did not fill in any clinical fields, SHAP values are estimated from typical clinical ranges. For accurate SHAP values, fill in the patient's actual data.

Card 4: 💬 Clinical Textual Justification

A plain-English paragraph that summarises the AI's reasoning. It is tailored to the risk level and explains:

  • What the AI observed in the image (tissue patterns, boundaries, discolouration)
  • What the findings suggest clinically
  • What the recommended next step is

If you filled in clinical parameters, a grid of those values also appears in this card for reference, showing exactly what data was used.

How the AI Works

A plain-English explanation of the technology — no jargon required.

Summary in one sentence: TBS sends your foot photo to a trained neural network (a type of AI that has "learned" what DFUs look like from thousands of images), which classifies the image and sends back a risk score and heatmap.

🌐
Frontend — What you see in the browser
Built with Next.js + React. This is the visual interface — the buttons, upload box, result cards, charts. It runs entirely in your browser. Styled with Tailwind CSS for a clean, responsive layout.
🔒
Authentication — Google Sign-In (NextAuth.js)
When you sign in, TBS uses NextAuth.js to talk to Google's secure login service. Your password never touches TBS's servers — Google handles it entirely and gives TBS a token saying "this person is who they say they are".
⚙️
API Layer — The bridge (Next.js API route)
The file /api/predict on the TBS server receives your image, forwards it to the AI backend, and returns the formatted result. If the AI backend is offline, it falls back to the simulation mode automatically.
🧠
AI Model — ResNet18 Neural Network
The brain of the system. ResNet18 is a convolutional neural network (CNN) originally designed for general image recognition and then retrained ("fine-tuned") on a dataset of diabetic foot images labelled as either Abnormal (Ulcer) or Normal (Healthy skin). It analyses each pixel region and outputs a probability score for each class.
🌡️
Grad-CAM — Visual Explanation Engine
After the ResNet18 model makes its prediction, a technique called Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping (Grad-CAM) traces back through the network to find which image regions were most "activated" (influential) for the decision. Those regions are drawn as a colour overlay on your photo.
🚇
ngrok Tunnel — Making the AI reachable from the internet
The AI model runs on a Google Colab notebook (a free cloud GPU). Colab computers are not normally reachable from the internet, so a service called ngrok creates a secure tunnel with a public URL (unillusive-unreally-sharolyn.ngrok-free.dev). The TBS server is configured with this URL to forward your images to the AI.
What is "transfer learning"? Instead of building an AI from scratch (which needs millions of images), the team started with ResNet18 which already "understands" shapes, textures, and edges from training on millions of general photos. Then they retrained its final layers on DFU images. This gives high accuracy even with a small medical dataset — like teaching an experienced radiologist a new speciality instead of teaching a complete beginner everything from zero.

Section 9

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers to the most common questions about Toe Be Sure.

Q: Do I need to install any app or software?
No. TBS runs entirely in your web browser. Just visit toe-be-sure.vercel.app on any device — phone, tablet, or computer.
Q: Why do I need to sign in with Google? Can't I use the app anonymously?
A Google sign-in is required to prevent misuse and protect the service. TBS only accesses your basic Google profile (name, email, photo) — no other Google data is read or stored.
Q: Are my photos stored permanently on your servers?
No. Images are transmitted for analysis only and are not saved to any database. The system processes them in memory and discards them immediately after the result is returned.
Q: The result says "⚡ Simulated" — what does that mean?
It means the live AI backend (the Google Colab server) is not currently running. TBS automatically falls back to a demonstration mode that generates a plausible-looking result. These simulated results are NOT based on your actual image and must not be used for any medical decisions.
Q: How accurate is the AI?
The underlying ResNet18 model was trained and validated on a curated DFU dataset. However, accuracy depends heavily on image quality, lighting, and angle. Real-world clinical accuracy has not been independently validated. Always treat the result as a screening tool, not a diagnosis.
Q: Can I use this on mobile (phone / tablet)?
Yes. The website is fully responsive and works on mobile browsers. You can take a photo directly on your phone and upload it from the camera roll. On mobile, navigation links are inside the ☰ hamburger menu.
Q: What if the analysis is taking too long or produces an error?
Wait a few seconds and try again. If the Colab backend has gone to sleep (it times out after inactivity), the system will fall back to simulation mode. A red warning box will appear if there is an unexpected error.
Q: What file formats are supported for the foot image?
JPG (JPEG), PNG, and WEBP are supported. Most phone camera photos are automatically in JPG or HEIC format — if your phone saves HEIC, convert it to JPG first, or use iOS's "Compatibility" camera mode.
Q: What does the confidence percentage mean?
It represents how certain the neural network is about its decision. A confidence of 91% means the AI's highest class probability was 91% — i.e., it is 91% confident the foot belongs to the predicted category. Scores above 80% are generally considered high-confidence.
Q: Can I switch between dark mode and light mode?
Yes — click the 🌙 (moon) icon in the top-right of the navigation bar to switch to dark mode. Click ☀️ (sun) to switch back to light mode. The setting lasts for your current session.

Section 10

Important Medical Disclaimer

Please read this section carefully before using Toe Be Sure for any health-related decision.

⚠️ This is NOT a medical device

Toe Be Sure is a clinical decision support prototype built for educational and research purposes. It has not been approved by any medical regulatory authority (such as the FDA, CE, or CDSCO) as a medical device. The results it produces must never replace the advice, diagnosis, or treatment recommendations of a qualified healthcare professional.

Always consult a doctor. If you or a patient has any foot wounds, discolouration, swelling, or pain, please seek medical attention regardless of the result shown by TBS. A Low-Risk result does not guarantee the absence of disease.

What TBS is intended for

  • Providing a quick first-pass screening to flag feet that may need further evaluation
  • Demonstrating explainable AI techniques (Grad-CAM, SHAP) in a medical context
  • Educational use by medical students, healthcare trainees, and researchers
  • Hackathon and academic demonstration of multi-modal deep learning

What TBS must NOT be used for

  • Replacing a clinical examination by a qualified doctor or nurse
  • Making any treatment or medication decisions
  • Diagnosing any medical condition or disease
  • Use in emergency or critical care situations
